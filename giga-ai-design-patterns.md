# Design Patterns: Websites I Like

---

# 1. Giga.ai — Enterprise AI, cinematic & premium

## Overview
Giga is an AI agent platform for enterprise support. Their site is a strong example of modern B2B SaaS design done with taste.

---

## Visual Design

### Hero with full-bleed photography
- Uses a **cinematic landscape photo** (mountains, mist) as the hero background — creates emotional resonance without being product-literal
- Photo is muted/desaturated enough to not compete with text
- The nature imagery signals scale, calm, and reliability — smart brand alignment for an "AI that talks like a human"

### Typography contrast: serif + sans-serif
- **Large serif font** for hero headlines ("AI that talks like a human.") — feels editorial, premium, human
- **Sans-serif** for body copy, nav, labels — functional and readable
- The serif/sans pairing creates hierarchy without needing size alone

### Dark → light → dark rhythm
- Hero: light (atmospheric photo)
- Stats + features sections: dark background (`#000` or near black)
- CTA/demo section: light again (white with soft pink gradient)
- This rhythm prevents visual fatigue and creates natural section breaks

---

## Layout Patterns

### Two-column feature layout
- Left: headline + description + CTA button
- Right: actual product screenshot or UI mockup
- Consistent across multiple sections — builds a familiar scanning pattern

### Sticky navigation
- Minimal: logo + 2 dropdown menus + sign in + CTA
- CTA ("Talk to us") is always visible — high conversion intent

### Stats displayed with authority
- Numbers like **98% deflection rate** and **99 supported languages** are typeset very large
- Placed in a grid, no explanation needed — the number does the work

---

## Content Patterns

### Announcement badge
- Small pill at the top of the hero: `● GIGA LAUNCHES BROWSER AGENT >`
- Creates urgency and signals momentum without being loud
- The `●` dot acts as a live/active indicator (subtle motion cue)

### Section labels as microcopy
- Small uppercase tags like `● CUSTOM AGENTS` or `● GET A PERSONALIZED DEMO` above section headings
- Orange dot color consistently used as an accent — creates visual system

### Progressive disclosure in feature steps
- "Create the agent → Define policies → Design the logic → Test and launch → Monitor and improve"
- Listed as expandable rows — user can scan the workflow without being overwhelmed
- The active step (Test and launch) shows detail + product screenshot

### Social proof via logo strip
- Postman, DoorDash, Capital.com, Afriex, Sendoso — placed right below the hero fold
- No testimonials needed — logos do the trust-building efficiently

---

## Product Screenshot Strategy
- Embeds **real-looking UI mockups** inside the page (not abstract illustrations)
- Screenshots are cropped into a browser/card frame — feels authentic, not staged
- The content inside screenshots is plausible and detailed (test results, pass rates, scenario names)
- This approach shows the product without requiring a live demo

---

## CTA Strategy
- One primary CTA throughout: **"Talk to us"** (not "Sign up free", not "Start trial")
- This signals enterprise positioning — they want qualified leads, not self-serve signups
- CTA button is pill-shaped, dark fill on light backgrounds, outline on dark

---

## Footer
- Clean multi-column layout: Product / Company / Resources
- Compliance badges (SOC2, ISO 42001, ISO 27001) displayed with a "Compliant" tag — directly addresses enterprise buyer concerns
- Minimal — no newsletter signup, no chat widget, no noise

---

## Key Takeaways

| Pattern | Why it works |
|---|---|
| Cinematic photo hero | Emotional, memorable, differentiates from typical SaaS |
| Serif headlines | Feels premium and human — aligned with brand promise |
| Dark section backgrounds | Creates focus and makes product screenshots pop |
| Large stat numbers | Instant credibility without reading |
| Single CTA ("Talk to us") | Clear conversion goal, enterprise-appropriate |
| Real product screenshots | Shows the actual product, builds trust |
| Orange dot microcopy labels | Creates visual system, signals activity |
| Compliance badges in footer | Directly addresses enterprise buyer anxieties |

# 2. Passionfroot.me — Creator-led GTM, playful & warm

## Overview
Passionfroot is an AI agent for creator marketing campaigns. Their site targets a creative, marketing-savvy audience — not enterprise IT — and the design reflects that completely. A strong contrast to Giga, both done well in their own lane.

---

## Visual Design

### 3D illustrated mascot
- A cute pink/purple fruit character anchors the hero — immediately communicates brand name and personality
- Not a stock illustration: it's a **3D-rendered character** that feels proprietary and ownable
- Placed bottom-right, partially cut off — feels alive, like it just walked into frame
- This alone makes the brand memorable. Most SaaS sites don't dare do this.

### AI-generated fantasy illustrations as section banners
- Instead of photography or flat color, they use **AI-generated illustrated cityscapes** (warm, dreamy, glowing)
- These feel fresh and on-brand — they sell "magic" and creativity, which fits a creator economy product
- The illustration style is consistent across sections, making it a visual system

### Night sky hero → cream content → illustrated banners
- Hero: deep purple/midnight blue (starfield background)
- Content sections: warm cream/off-white (`#f5f0eb` range)
- Section breaks: full-bleed illustrated banners
- The cream background is unusual for SaaS — feels editorial, not corporate

### Purple gradient side panels
- A soft purple gradient frames the left and right edges of the content area
- Creates a gentle "vignette" effect — draws the eye inward to the content
- Subtle but distinctive, adds depth without being distracting

---

## Layout Patterns

### Floating status cards around the hero prompt
- The hero shows an AI chat prompt ("Run a $25K campaign for our Q1 launch") with **floating status pills** scattered around it
- Each pill shows a completed step: "70 Contacted", "8 Confirmed", "37 live posts", "Budget Deployment"
- This visualizes the agent's work without a traditional feature list — shows the outcome, not the process
- Much more engaging than bullet points

### Full-screen testimonial section
- One quote takes the entire viewport, typeset very large in serif
- The last part of the quote ("I found Passionfroot.") appears to fade/animate in — creates a scroll-triggered reveal
- No testimonial carousel, no grid of quotes — just ONE, treated like a billboard
- Forces the reader to sit with the words

### Network visualization for social proof
- Instead of just showing response rate stats, they visualize it: creator avatars orbiting a central node with positive chat bubbles ("This looks great", "I'm interested!", "Count me in")
- Makes the abstract stat (8x response rate) feel tangible and human

### Sticky "Watch the trailer" button
- A persistent video CTA floats at the bottom of the screen throughout the scroll
- Small, unobtrusive — thumbnail + text
- Serves visitors who prefer watching over reading, without interrupting the main flow

---

## Content Patterns

### Dual audience navigation
- Nav clearly splits: **"For Brands"** | **"For Creators"**
- Immediately tells each visitor they're in the right place
- Smart segmentation — same product, two very different use cases and messaging

### Two CTAs in the hero: self-serve + video
- **"Start for free"** (primary, white pill) + **"Watch the trailer"** (secondary, outline)
- Covers two buyer types: the ready-to-try and the needs-convincing
- "Start for free" signals PLG/self-serve — no sales call needed (contrast to Giga's "Talk to us")

### Anti-pain copy
- "No spreadsheets, no guesswork, no chaos" — directly names what the product eliminates
- "Work with creators who actually respond" — addresses the #1 frustration before explaining features
- This pattern (name the pain, then show the solution) appears across multiple sections

### Comparative stats with context
- **8x response rates** *vs. cold outreach* / **3x faster turnaround** *vs. manual campaigns*
- The comparison qualifier makes the stat more credible and meaningful
- Small grey subtext — doesn't oversell, lets the number speak

### Platform logo strip as trust signal
- X, beehiiv, Substack, LinkedIn, YouTube, Instagram, TikTok
- For creator economy buyers, this is critical — "does it work where my creators are?"
- Different trust signal than enterprise logos — it's about ecosystem, not client names

---

## Product Screenshot Strategy
- Screenshots are shown mid-workflow with real-looking data (creator names, follower counts, campaign metrics)
- Multiple screenshots **overlap and layer** rather than sitting flat — feels like a living product
- Outreach emails shown are personalized and specific — demonstrates the quality of the AI output, not just that it exists

---

## Key Takeaways

| Pattern | Why it works |
|---|---|
| 3D mascot character | Ownable, memorable, communicates brand personality instantly |
| Fantasy illustrated banners | Fresh, on-brand, avoids generic stock photography |
| Floating status cards in hero | Shows AI workflow outcomes visually, not as a feature list |
| Full-screen single testimonial | Forces attention, feels confident and editorial |
| Dual audience nav | Removes friction — both user types feel addressed immediately |
| "Start for free" vs "Talk to us" | PLG self-serve signal vs enterprise sales signal — different positioning |
| Anti-pain copy | Names the frustration before selling the solution |
| Comparative stats with context | More believable than raw numbers alone |
| Sticky trailer CTA | Serves video-preferring visitors without interrupting the page flow |

---

## Giga vs Passionfroot: The Core Contrast

| | Giga | Passionfroot |
|---|---|---|
| Audience | Enterprise buyers | Marketing teams / creators |
| Hero visual | Cinematic landscape photo | Illustrated night sky + 3D mascot |
| Primary CTA | "Talk to us" | "Start for free" |
| Trust signals | Company logos + compliance badges | Platform logos + testimonials |
| Tone | Calm, authoritative | Playful, warm, human |
| Illustration style | None — real photography | AI-generated fantasy cityscapes |
| Conversion model | Sales-led | Product-led |

Both sites are excellent — the lesson is that **great design is audience-matched**, not formula-based.

# 3. Anthropic.com & Claude.com — Research credibility meets product clarity

## Overview
Anthropic (the research company) and Claude (the product) are two separate sites with two different jobs. Anthropic feels like a serious institution. Claude feels like a polished product. Both share the same visual DNA but are tuned differently — and that distinction is itself a design lesson.

---

## Visual Design

### The warm cream baseline
- Both sites use a warm off-white/cream background (`~#f0ede8`) as the default canvas
- Not pure white — it's softer, more paper-like, less clinical
- Everything on top of it (black type, dark cards) has natural contrast without feeling harsh
- This is a deliberate step away from generic SaaS white — feels considered

### Serif + sans-serif system, used consistently
- Large serif for mission/vision statements: "At Anthropic, we build AI to serve humanity's long-term well-being."
- Sans-serif for nav, labels, body — no confusion about which does what
- The serif signals long-term thinking and intellectual seriousness; the sans-serif keeps it usable
- Same pairing as Giga and Passionfroot — this is becoming the dominant pattern for AI companies that want to feel human

### Dark sections for enterprise/impact moments
- Customer stat cards: deep black background, logo top-left, massive percentage center (`89%`, `98%`)
- The darkness creates a stage — makes the numbers feel weighty and final
- The same company logos that would look routine on a white background become authoritative on black

### Hand-drawn / sketchy icons on pricing page
- The two enterprise pricing tiers use simple line-art icons (small shop, building)
- These are deliberately imperfect and human — reduces the intimidation of enterprise pricing
- Unusual choice for a serious product — works because the rest of the page is clean enough to absorb the playfulness

---

## Layout Patterns

### Card grid for content (Anthropic.com)
- Latest releases shown as equal-weight cards: title, subtitle, metadata (DATE + CATEGORY as label/value pairs), CTA button at the bottom
- Metadata is typeset as small caps labels (`DATE`, `CATEGORY`) aligned in a two-column mini-grid inside the card — very precise, editorial
- All three cards same height — signals all content is equally important, no hierarchy imposed

### Content list as editorial index
- Below the cards: article titles left-aligned, category right-aligned, separated by horizontal rules
- Feels like a magazine index or academic journal contents page
- Extremely readable for scanning — lets the content titles do all the work

### Dual navigation bar (Claude.com)
- Primary nav at the top + secondary contextual breadcrumb bar below it
- The second bar also has an **"Explore here ▼"** dropdown on the far right — a contextual "you are here / go deeper" affordance
- Rare pattern — very helpful on a large site where users land mid-funnel

### Feature list with icon + two-column text
- Section: small icon left, feature name + bold text center-left, description center-right
- Horizontal rules separate each row
- Clean and dense — communicates a lot without cards or boxes
- The floating customer case study card overlaid on the dark background adds a depth layer without breaking the grid

### Customer stories as a filterable media library
- Customers page has a full left sidebar with filters: Sort by, Industry, Product, Size, Partner, Geography
- Main area: 3-column card grid, each card is logo on solid background + headline below
- Search bar + Grid/List toggle at the top
- This treats customer stories like a proper content product — not just a logo wall
- Incredibly powerful for enterprise buyers doing research: "show me companies like mine"

### Pricing: side-by-side self-serve vs sales-assisted
- Two cards, same visual weight — no "recommended" badge forcing a choice
- Left: concrete price (`$20/seat/month`), long CTA ("Get Enterprise plan")
- Right: no price, relationship-focused ("Work with Anthropic's sales team"), CTA is "Contact sales"
- Floating "Quiz: Which plan is right for you?" element off to the right — reduces decision anxiety without interrupting the cards

---

## Content Patterns

### Mission statement as design element
- "At Anthropic, we build AI to serve humanity's long-term well-being." is typeset large, left-aligned, bold serif — treated like a headline, not a tagline
- Not buried in an About page — it's on the homepage, in the content area
- This is rare: most companies hide their mission. Putting it in the product flow signals confidence and differentiation

### Customer stat cards: logo + number + one line of context
- Pattern: `[Company logo]` → `[huge %]` → `[one sentence of context]`
- Example: Zapier logo → `89%` → "employee adoption, 800+ AI agents deployed, 10x app usage growth"
- The one sentence does a lot of work: it answers "89% of what?" immediately, with specifics
- Carousel lets them show multiple customers without overwhelming the layout

### Customer story headlines as benefit statements
- Not "Stripe uses Claude Code" — but "Stripe deploys Claude Code to 1,370 engineers with zero-configuration enterprise rollout"
- The headline itself is the case study summary — you don't need to click to understand the value
- This respects the reader's time and builds trust faster

### "Quiz: Which plan is right for you?" micro-interaction
- A small floating element on the pricing page
- Takes the pressure off the binary choice — gives buyers an escape hatch
- Signals user-centricity: Anthropic is helping you decide, not pushing you to the more expensive option

---

## Key Takeaways

| Pattern | Why it works |
|---|---|
| Warm cream background | Softer than pure white, more considered, less generic SaaS |
| Mission statement as content | Signals confidence; differentiates from companies that hide their values |
| Metadata as mini label/value grid in cards | Editorial precision, easy to scan |
| Content list (title left, category right) | Magazine index feel — great for research-mode visitors |
| Dark background for stat cards | Makes numbers feel final and authoritative |
| Dual nav bar + "Explore here" | Contextual orientation on a large site |
| Customer stories as filterable library | Respects enterprise buyers doing peer research |
| Benefit-forward story headlines | Delivers the case study value without requiring a click |
| Hand-drawn icons on pricing | Humanizes enterprise, reduces intimidation |
| "Quiz: Which plan is right?" | Reduces decision anxiety, signals user-centricity |
